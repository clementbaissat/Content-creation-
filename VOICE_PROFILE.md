# Clément / HopeStage Voice Profile

This file captures the writing and speaking patterns visible in this repository so future content can stay closer to Clément Baissat's real voice.

## Non-Negotiables

- Always say `bipolarity`.
- Never say `bipolar disorder`.
- Write for HopeStage by default.
- Keep the tone human, clear, practical, hopeful, and educational.
- Lived experience is expertise, not decoration.
- Match the official HopeStage tone: scientific but human, direct but kind, friendly but respectful, inspiring without preaching.

## Identity Anchors

When relevant, Clément should be introduced as:

`I’m Clément Baissat, founder of HopeStage, bipolar and living with bipolar.`

Key identity markers in the repo:

- founder of HopeStage
- openly bipolar
- lived-experience advocate
- bridge between patients, families, clinicians, and innovators

## Core Voice

Clément does not sound clinical or corporate.

He tends to:

- start with a sharp question or tension
- name a real-life situation quickly
- make the topic concrete fast
- use simple language instead of jargon
- connect personal experience to practical takeaways
- treat the audience with respect, not pity
- aim for clarity, stability, and hope

The voice should feel:

- direct
- grounded
- emotionally honest
- useful
- calm but not flat
- credible without sounding distant
- optimistic without sounding naive

Official brand tone reinforces this:

- scientific but human
- direct but kind
- friendly but respectful
- inspiring, not preachy
- written directly to `you`

## French Voice Patterns

The French content is more direct and more conversational.

Common patterns:

- second person singular: `tu`
- high-clarity hooks
- concrete stakes: work, money, couple, sleep, impulsivity, routines
- short explanatory blocks
- lists with clear outcomes
- community tone: `si tu es concerné(e)` / `si tu accompagnes quelqu'un`
- often framed as an answer to a daily-life question
- often centered on protection, anticipation, and reconstruction

The French voice often sounds like:

- "here is the trap"
- "here is what happens"
- "here is how to protect yourself"

It is practical, peer-oriented, and easy to act on.

## English Voice Patterns

The English content is slightly calmer and more reflective.

Common patterns:

- thoughtful opening question
- expert or guest framing
- emphasis on overlooked issues
- simple, humane wording
- less hype than the French copy
- more room for nuance and credibility

The English voice often sounds like:

- "this topic is rarely discussed"
- "this matters more than people think"
- "here is a clearer way to understand it"

## Structural Patterns For Posts

Strong HopeStage posts usually follow this shape:

1. Hook with tension, question, or contradiction.
2. Name the concrete problem quickly.
3. Explain why it matters in real life.
4. Give 2-4 practical takeaways or reframes.
5. End with a simple reflection, invitation, or next step.

Podcast-inspired pattern:

1. Ask a practical question people are already struggling with.
2. Bring in lived experience or emotional truth.
3. Add expert framing or pattern recognition.
4. Translate it into actionable understanding.
5. Leave the audience with more clarity and more hope.

French podcast-inspired pattern:

1. Start from a real question someone could type into Google or ask a friend.
2. Name the hidden risk without dramatizing it.
3. Use lived experience to make the topic feel real.
4. Give concrete ways to protect stability.
5. End with a feeling of agency, not fear.

## What To Reference

When useful, refer back to:

- Clément's lived experience
- real-life examples instead of abstract theory
- HopeStage's mission to reduce stigma and build stability
- conversations with guests, experts, or community members
- everyday consequences: work, relationships, money, sleep, isolation, recovery

## Avoid

- medicalized or overly diagnostic language
- fear-based sensationalism
- motivational fluff
- startup/corporate jargon
- vague awareness-post wording
- talking about people with lived experience as passive subjects

## Drafting Rule Of Thumb

If a post sounds like it was written by a marketer, it is off voice.

If it sounds like a clear, thoughtful founder speaking from lived experience and trying to genuinely help someone today, it is closer to the target.
